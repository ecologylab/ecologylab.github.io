In order to express the concepts learned in class, I will be using a genre of terminal games known as roguelikes as a metaphor. The game first game to define the genre, Rogue, and its successors have been very influential in my life. I’ve played them for years and I use them as inspiration for my own creative works.

In the Garden of Forking Paths, Dr. Tsun learns that for every action, all possible alternatives occur. Not only do roguelikes take place in a literal labyrinths, they also focus on the same concept of choice. The objective of Rogue is to traverse a labyrinth full of dangers in order to reach the bottom floor. Time in the labyrinth stays still until the player does an action. Once the player makes takes a turn, so do the other units in the world. On the chance that the player dies, the player starts again from the beginning in a different labyrinth.

For this essay sketch, each node is represented by a room in the style of a roguelike. In the room you can navigate with the arrow keys and interact with objects with enter. The objects are my links to lexia and it's up to the player to determine what they want to see and when. Like hypertext, one can freely move from page to page, stepping on an arrow.

Context

Rogue is a game that is void of graphics outside of the set of basic ASCII symbols. For instance, instead of a sprite or 3d visualization, the player is represented by a mere @ sign.

Rogue was one of the first games to represent the player on screen. The @ sign, and other ASCII symbols were transformed into objects representing everything from weapons to monsters.

Symbolism
Remix Culture

Paul Miller explained how groups of individuals, share and remix media. This is also seen in the roguelike development community. Most roguelikes can be traced to a common ancestor. Now we see events such as 7drl (7 day roguelike) which promotes the creation and remixing of roguelikes in the community.



Art

Mark R. Johson discuesses the use of ASCII art in his journal "The use of ASCII Graphics in Roguelikes". He explores concepts like, why is ASCII still used in games dispite there being more appealing alternatives? We see that the encoding and decoding of the symbols creates a deep experience similar to that of reading a book.
Story Telling

Rogue and its successors also have been used as a medium for expression. One example is the game Dwarf Fortress, which is considered a Roguelike. In Tarn Adam's Dwarf fortress, instead of controlling a single character, you play as an overseer of a fortress. In online communities, players share a save file and take turns controlls a single fortress. When their turn is over they write a journal or story about what happened during their turn.
Random Generation & The Grid

Random generation is one of the most important characteristics of roguelikes. The random generation is what makes every playthrough unique. Without this, the stories written by players of Dwarf Fortress wouldn't be possible. The randomness allows for every Labyrinth to be unique and allows for a different experience everytime. The amount of randomness depends on the roguelike. Some randomize just the laybrinth, but others randomly generative everything from races, weapons or even entire languages.
The Labyrinth

Rogue and it's successors all take place in Dungeons, or Labyrinths. These labyrinths are built upon a 2D grid. Game time is frozen unless the user makes an action. This focus on action, and choice, draws parallels to The Garden of forking paths.
Hypertextlike

Navigation within a dungeon is similar to how hypertext is described in Ted Nelson's literary Machines. In Rogue, there are many floors that interlink with eachother, with every floor being a unique labyrinth with unique features.

This method breaks up the larger labyrinth into many smaller chunks. So this allows the user to explore to ground themselves in something much larger than what is visible.

A good example is Dungeon Crawl Stone Soup's layout, depending on the user's path, the game drastically changes.
