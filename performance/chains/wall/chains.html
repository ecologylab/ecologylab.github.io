Coded Messages: CHAINS explores an intercultural interface ecosystem by juxtaposing the drum language performance of the Ewe people of Ghana, West Africa, with the advertisements of multinational corporate society. The performance strategies of CHAINS are based on the compositional and media integration aesthetics of Ewe performances -- which blend drumming, dancing, singing, costumes and architecture in a unified expressive form -- and avant garde performance strategies from environmental theater and new music. Like the site specific performances in the remote village of Anyako and the historic slave trade Castle at Cape Coast, which preceeded it, the CHAINS WWW site builds its context into the delivery of its messages.

The CHAINS performances were featured in the 1994 Pan Africa Theater Festival, PANAFEST. The WWW form of Coded Messages: CHAINS was awarded honorable mention in the 1996 Prix Ars Electronica. The CHAINS site is ranked in "The Top 5% of all Web Sites," by Lycos, and four stars by Magellan and NetGuide. The "Annotated Coded Messages: CHAINS" is featured in the Leonardo on-line journal of MIT Press.

Visitors can find audio, video and photos from the performances, interpretive translations and background information, and the CHAINS Grafitti Wall, where you can add your own images, text, and links to the site. Now in its second year on the Internet, the CHAINS web site draws approximately one hundred visitors per day.

CHAINS also reflects both the powerful potential and the limitations in sharing which are inherent when empathetic post-colonial interfaces between cultures originate in the center and seek the margins. With the voyeuristic power of the computer desktop, the visitor can access all, but can only translate some coded messages. So, too, with the economic position of Americans, the instigators of the CHAINS collaboration opened some borders, while others remained sealed in another zone of 100% multinational authentic experience.
